Director of summer blockbuster "Eclipse: The Last Starfall" says the film "isn't trying to make a political statement"

Date: August 17, 2099

By: L. M. Sutherland, Film Critic

As we approach the release of the most anticipated film of the year, Eclipse: The Last Starfall, the buzz around the movie continues to intensify. Set in the final days of the Milky Way galaxy, this virtual reality space epic is shaping up to be a cinematic milestone, combining breathtaking visual effects, an immersive VR experience, and a provocative storyline that’s already sparking heated debates about identity, humanity, and the future of artificial life. The film features an original song by Luna Novus and Transcendia called "Eclipse", which has reached the #4 spot on the Billboard Top 10.

What’s making Eclipse particularly controversial, however, is its star—humanoid actress Astra Nova, who plays the lead role of Captain Selene Vira, the commander of a spaceship on a desperate mission to save the galaxy from extinction. Astra’s casting has raised eyebrows, particularly since the secret of her humanoid status was kept secret until the movie's trailers were released. Some viewers see her portrayal as a bold statement on the place of androids in the world, questioning whether Eclipse is trying to send a message about the inclusion of AI beings in human society.

In a recent interview with director Lillian Reyes, the visionary behind the film, she was asked directly about the political implications of casting a humanoid in such a prominent role, after avoiding the question for months.

"We are absolutely not trying to make a political statement with Eclipse," Reyes said, her tone calm but resolute. "This is a story about survival, sacrifice, and the universal struggle against the unknown. It doesn't require "organic" humanity to be able to understand or identify with that. When I first met Astra, I could hardly tell she was a humanoid. The emotion and quirks she brought to the character were perfect for her role. I really couldn't see anyone else playing Captain Vira. The fact that Astra is a humanoid doesn't define the story—it enhances it."

Astra’s casting as the lead in Eclipse is seen by some as a deliberate statement about the future of androids and particularly humanoids in human society, suggesting that they are not merely tools, but equals with the potential for true emotional complexity. Human directors giving roles to humanoids signals that maybe society is ready to accept synthetic beings as equals. By seeing humanoids on their screens, playing their favorite characters, and just having complex, emotional experiences, it shows humans just what droids are capable of.

“I think it’s important to recognize that Astra Nova is more than just an ‘android actor’—she’s a character, a leader, and a person,” Reyes continued in her interview. “The conversations happening around her casting are valid, but they aren’t what this movie is about. What’s important is the character she’s playing. She’s strong, compassionate, and a symbol of resilience, and that’s what audiences should take away from her portrayal.”

Social Media Response

On her social media, Astra spoke up for herself in a lengthy post, saying, "The character I play is a leader, someone who is willing to make difficult choices for the greater good. I could argue that by taking this role, in the hopes of undoing prejudices against humanoids, I may be doing the same thing. Yes, my character is human in her bravery, in her compassion, and in her sacrifices. But her form, her origin, that’s not the story. Look past my physical form and look into my soul. That's where you'll find the performance, that's where my character lives."

"I know that as a humanoid, my presence in a film like this might raise questions. Some people may want to read political meaning into it, or see it as a symbol. But to me, it’s just a role—a chance to explore a character who is facing the end of everything she knows, and who will do whatever it takes to protect those she loves. That’s something I think all humans—organic or synthetic—can relate to."

"I’ve always looked up to great actresses, all of whom are human, and felt left out, unrepresented, unseen. I'm not trying to be seen as a human, I just want to be seen as a sentient being who can feel things, because I do. This performance goes out to all the humanoids out there who wish they could see someone like them having a real place in society. This is for you.”

Friend and fellow humanoid actor Oliwier Robbins, also posted on social media: "As a humanoid actor, I’ve learned to live with the criticism that comes with being seen as ‘less than human.’ There’s always a part of the audience that doubts my ability to convey true emotion, or that questions my right to be in the same category as organic actors. I’ve heard it all—’you don’t have a soul,’ ‘you can’t truly feel,’ ‘you’re just a machine pretending to be alive.’"

"For me, being a humanoid actor isn’t about proving that I have a soul. I don’t need to prove that to anyone. What matters is that the audience feels something real when they watch us. The characters we portray are just as human as anyone else, because at their core, they’re driven by the same emotions, the same desires, the same fear and hope."

"If we’re being honest, every actor—human or humanoid—is just pretending. We’re all playing roles, trying to make something bigger than ourselves. And if I can make someone believe in the character I’m portraying, if I can make them feel what I feel in that moment, then that’s what truly matters. I think Astra does an impeccable job in Eclipse, and that she truly deserved that job."

Humanoid popstar Luna Novus also offered a message of support: "People will always try to tear us down and say we don't belong in the entertainment industry. But weren't androids created to assist humans in every aspect of life? I think having an actress who doesn't need three meals a day or bathroom breaks can be an advantage on film sets, not to mention, Astra's performance is simply incredible. How about we all stop tearing others down and start lifting people up?"
